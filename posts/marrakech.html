Getting Gloriously Lost in Marrakech's Souks

June 18, 2026 • 6 min read

I gave up on my map of the Marrakech medina about eleven minutes in, somewhere between a stall selling nothing but tagine lids and one selling nothing but doorknobs. The souks aren't really built to be navigated, I've decided — they're built to be surrendered to, a tangle of covered alleyways where shafts of light fall through gaps in the reed roofing and land on pyramids of saffron, cascades of leather babouches, and more brass lanterns than I have ever seen in one place trying to exist at once.

A teenager selling mint tea glasses took pity on my obviously lost expression and, without being asked, walked me two full turns deeper into the maze to a lantern stall run by a man named Youssef, because I'd made the mistake of mentioning I liked the copper ones. What followed was forty-five minutes of tea, theater, and negotiation that felt less like a transaction and more like a small performance we were both enjoying. I started at half his opening price out of principle. We met somewhere in the middle, both of us grinning like we'd won.

"You are not buying a lamp," Youssef told me, wrapping it in newspaper with the focus of a surgeon. "You are buying the story of how annoying you were to bargain with. That is worth more."

By early evening the medina exhales into Jemaa el-Fna, the main square, which by day is fairly ordinary and by night becomes something closer to organized chaos — orange juice carts lit by bare bulbs, drummers circled by clapping strangers, and a man with a snake draped over his shoulders who made eye contact with me specifically, which I chose to interpret as a challenge and did not accept. I ate dinner at a rooftop table overlooking the whole spectacle, watching smoke rise off a hundred grills at once, and understood why every guidebook fails to actually describe this place. It isn't a sight. It's a sound and a smell and a slight sense of delighted panic, all at the same time.

I never did find my way back to that lantern stall on my last day, despite trying for an hour. I like to think Youssef would find that fitting. The souks gave me exactly what I came for and made sure I couldn't just casually get it again — which, I suspect, was always the point.
