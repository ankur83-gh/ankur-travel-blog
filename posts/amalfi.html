Cliffside and Carefree: Driving the Amalfi Coast

June 9, 2026 • 5 min read

Nobody warns you that renting a small blue Fiat and driving the Amalfi coast road yourself will turn you into a slightly different, slightly braver person by the end of it. The road itself is barely two lanes wide, stitched into the cliffside in a series of switchbacks that a local at my guesthouse described, with real affection, as "a road that hates you a little." Every third turn opens onto a view so absurd — turquoise water, pastel houses stacked like they're daring gravity — that I had to stop just to remember I was supposed to be watching the road.

I stopped in Positano first, mostly because I ran out of nerve to keep driving through it, and spent an hour just climbing its endless staircases past lemon trees heavy enough to bend their own branches. A woman selling limoncello from a stand no bigger than a phone booth handed me a sample before I'd even asked, and then a second one "for balance," and then told me the entire family recipe unprompted, including the exact number of days the lemon peel needs to steep — a number she made me swear not to repeat, which I'm still honoring.

"You drive too carefully," she laughed, watching me white-knuckle my way back to the parking lot. "The road is the whole point. Let it scare you a little."

Further along, I found a cliffside restaurant in a town too small to be in most guidebooks, where the owner sat me at a table literally hanging over the water and brought dishes I hadn't ordered because, in his words, "the menu is more of a suggestion." I ate the best plate of scialatielli of my life while a boat trip to Capri's Blue Grotto sat unbooked on my phone, because I couldn't bring myself to leave the table. Some days the plan just isn't as good as staying put.

I did eventually make it to Capri the next morning, ducking through the low grotto entrance in a rowboat with six other slightly nervous tourists, the water glowing an impossible electric blue around us. But it's the limoncello stand and that hanging table I think about most. The coast rewards the people willing to stop the car — even on a road that hates you a little.
